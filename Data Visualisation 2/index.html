Introduction

The Olympics, otherwise known as the Olympic Games, are international sporting events that include both Summer and Winter sports. The first Olympic Games was held on 6 April 1896 in Athens, and the most recent Olympic Games was the postponed 2020 Summer Olympics held on 23 July 2021 in Tokyo.

Thousands of athletes from over 200 nations participate in the Olympics, which is typically held every 4 years, alternating between Summer and Winter Olympics every 2 years. Before the 1992 Olympic Games, they were held every 4 years, with no distinction between Summer and Winter sports.

Due to World War 1, there were no Olympic Games held between 1912 - 1920, and due to World War 2, there were no Olympic Games held between 1936 - 1948.

This visualisation will identify how the height, weight, and age are distributed across different events and sports, as well as visualise how these trends have developed over Olympic history. The number of medals that a typical athlete from each country wins in the Olympics can also be identified, along with the total number of medals of each type won by each country. How physical characteristics of Olympic athletes in different sports have changed over the years

The bubble chart below depicts the relationship between the average height, weight, and age of athletes in all Olympic events throughout the years.

In addition to the two filters below, the chart is pannable and zoomable (double click to reset), the items in the legend can also be clicked to highlight a particular sport. The maximum and minimum average weight, height, and age for athletes in each event has also been annotated.

In general, there appears to be a linear relationship between the average height and average weight, which indicates that athletes across most sports typically maintain a healthy BMI (Body Mass Index).

In Summer Olympics, for each height, the events with the average heaviest athletes are typically Weightlifting and Wrestling. While the events with the average lightest athletes are typically Taekwondo and Gymnastics. For each weight, the events with the average tallest athletes are typically Basketball and Volleyball. While the events with the average shortest athletes are typically Weightlifting. The events with average oldest athletes are typically Equestrianism. While the events with the average youngest athletes are typically Gymnastics.

In Winter Olympics, for each height the events with the average heaviest athletes are typically Bobsleigh. While the events with the average lightest athletes are typically Figure Skating. For each weight, the events with the average tallest athletes are typically Bobsleigh and Ice Hockey. While the events with the average shortest athletes are typically Figure Skating. The events with the average oldest athletes are typically Curling. While the events with the average youngest athletes are typically Figure Skating.

These findings are very consistent over the years.

Medals won by the athletes for their countries

The map below shows the number of medals won by each country, normalised by the number of athletes from that country who participated in the Olympic Games.

There are no data for some countres, and those countries are greyed out on the map.

As clearly shown, Russia has the highest proportion of medals per athlete, at 0.338. With the United States of America behind at 0.299. Interestingly, and perhaps surprisingly, particular smaller countries such as Pakistan, Germany, and Norway have a higher proportion of medals per athlete than China or Australia.

The map can be used as a filter to select particular countries we may wish to compare raw medal counts with.

By selecting and comparing the countries discussed earlier, it is obvious that the United States of America has won the most medals overall, having gained more Gold, Silver, and Bronze medals than any other country. Russia is second, and Germany is following closely in third place. Though the population of China is more than 50 times than that of Australia, Australia has had both more athletes participate, and more medals won from the Olympic Games.

Conclusion

Overall, many interesting trends and information could be drawn from 120 years of data in this international sporting event known as the Olympic Games.

Through analysing the relationship of average height, weight, and age of athletes in Olympic events, there is clearly a positive relationship between average weight and height, which suggests that all athletes across different sports maintain a certain BMI.

Summer and Winter Olympics needed to be analysed separately as they cover different sports, but the sports with minimum and maximum average physical characteristics of athletes has remained similar throughout all 120 years of Olympic history.

Through analysis of the map, the country with the highest proportion of medals per athlete was identified as Russia, and found that a few small countries out-performed their larger rivals.

The bar chart allows the total raw medal count to be compared between countries, and it became clear that the United States of America had the most overall medals won, as well as most Bronze, Silver, and Gold medals won individually.

Lastly, the line charts allow us to identify which range of weight, height, and age in a particular sport leads to the most Bronze, Silver, Gold, and no medals won. It also provides eye-opening information in identifying the optimal physical characteristic range to win Gold.
